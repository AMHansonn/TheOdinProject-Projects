Simple Sourdough Starter

Description

This is a supposedly foolproof technique to ensure a strong and vibrant sourdough starter. It is a perfect choice for beginners and expeienced bakers alike!

Ingredients

  • 6 cups (720 g) of organic all-purpose flour
  • 3 cups (360 g) of organic whole wheat flour
  • Water

Equipment

  • Baker's Scale
  • Offset Spatula. Preferably silicone, as wood may harbor bacteria and metal may have a reaction with your starter (oh no!)
  • Glass Jar

Steps

  1. Mix 6 cups of all-purpose flour with 3 cups of whole-wheat flour and store it in an air-tight container. This is what we will feed our starters with!
  2. DAY 1: Add 1 cup (120g) flour and 1/2 cup (120g) water to a clean jar. Stir thouroughly and cover the jar with a lid or breathable cloth secured with a rubber band. Use another rubber band or a dry-erase marker to mark the level of your (soon-to-be) starter. Place the container in a warm and dark location for 24 hours.
    NOTE: If you can't find a dry-erase marker today, you can use a sharpie today. Later, you can write over that sharpie with a dry erase marker and both marks will wipe right off.
  3. DAY 2: Give the starter a stir to help incorporate some air into it. Cover and let it rest for another 24 hours in a warm, dark area.
  4. DAYS 3 THRU 7: Every 24 hours, discard all but 2 tablespoons of starter and feed it with 1/2 cup (60g) of flour and 1/4 cup (60 g) of water. Mix thoroughly until combined. Repeat this routine of discarding and feeding for 7 to 10 days or until your starter becomes active, bubbly, and doubles in size within 4 to 12 hours of feeding.
    NOTE: You don't have to throw away your starter! You can use it in place of flour and water in literally any recipe that calls for flour and water!
Tips and Troubleshooting
  • If your starter seems sluggish after the 4th day, begin stirring in 2 tbsp of flour with 2 tsp of water into the starter 12 hours after feeding it. This can help give it a little boost until next feeding.
  • It is recommended to continue feeding starter daily for the first two weeks after you've gotten it established in order to keep building up the yeast and bacteria to provide you with a very robust and mature starter.
  • After your starter is active, if you want to take a break for a little while or if you're going on holiday, you can put your sourdough in the fridge. It can last indefinitely in the fridge, but it is reccomended to feed it every week or two at a minimum for optimal health and quicker baking prep.
  • Little Spoon Farm has a good sourdough maintenance article that goes over feeding schedules, temperature control, further troubleshooting, and flavor development.
